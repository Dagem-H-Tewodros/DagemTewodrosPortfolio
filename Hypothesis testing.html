A/B Testing in Insurance: How Gender Impacts Claims, Severity, and Profitability

A Data-Driven Analysis Using Weighted Hypothesis Testing

Introduction

In the competitive landscape of insurance, understanding what drives claims and profitability across different customer segments is crucial for strategic decision-making. Are certain geographic regions inherently riskier? Do gender-based differences in driving behavior translate to measurable impacts on claim patterns? These questions aren't just academically interesting—they have direct implications for premium pricing, risk assessment, and market strategy.

This analysis presents a rigorous statistical investigation into insurance risk factors using advanced A/B testing methodologies. Rather than relying on simple comparisons that might be confounded by other variables, we employ propensity score weighting to ensure fair, apples-to-apples comparisons across three critical dimensions:

  1. Geographic Risk: Gauteng vs. Western Cape provinces
  2. Micro-Geographic Patterns: Specific zip codes (1002 vs. 1006)
  3. Demographic Factors: Male vs. Female policyholders

Through this analysis, we'll uncover statistically significant differences in claim frequency, severity, and profitability, providing actionable insights for insurance underwriting and pricing strategies.

The Business Problem

Insurance companies face a fundamental challenge: how to price policies that accurately reflect risk while remaining competitive in the market. Mispricing can lead to two costly outcomes:

  • Underpricing high-risk segments → Loss of profitability, potential insolvency
  • Overpricing low-risk segments → Loss of competitive advantage, customer churn

Traditional actuarial methods often struggle to isolate the true causal effect of specific risk factors because customer populations differ across multiple dimensions simultaneously. For instance, if we observe higher claims in Gauteng compared to Western Cape, is this due to geographic factors (traffic density, road conditions) or because Gauteng has a different mix of vehicle types, driver demographics, or coverage selections?

This is where causal inference through propensity score weighting becomes invaluable.

Methodology: Propensity Score Weighting for Causal Inference

Why Not Simple Comparisons?

A naive approach would simply compare average claims between groups. However, this fails to account for confounding variables factors that differ between groups and also affect outcomes.

For example, if Gauteng has more luxury vehicles with comprehensive coverage compared to Western Cape, any difference in claims might reflect vehicle type rather than geographic risk.

The Propensity Score Solution

Propensity score weighting creates a "pseudo-randomized" comparison by:

  1. Modeling the probability that a policy falls into treatment vs. control group based on observable characteristics
  2. Weighting observations inversely to their probability of group membership
  3. Balancing covariates so both groups have similar distributions of confounding variables

This mimics the balance we'd achieve in a randomized controlled trial, allowing us to isolate the true effect of the factor being tested.

Key Metrics

We evaluate three critical insurance metrics:

  1. Claim Frequency
  2. Claim Severity
  3. Margin

Statistical Testing Framework

For each comparison, we:

  1. Fit a logistic regression model to predict group membership (treatment assignment)
  2. Generate propensity scores (predicted probabilities)
  3. Calculate inverse propensity weights: weight = 1/p for treatment, 1/(1-p) for control
  4. Compute weighted means for each metric in both groups
  5. Perform weighted t-tests to assess statistical significance (α = 0.05)

Creating Key Metrics

We engineer our three core metrics at the policy level:


# Binary indicator: did this policy have any claims?
df['HasClaim'] = np.where(df['TotalClaims'] > 0, 1, 0)

# Claim frequency: 1 if claim occurred, 0 otherwise
df['ClaimFrequency'] = df['HasClaim']

# Claim severity: amount of claim when claim occurred (NaN otherwise)
df['ClaimSeverity'] = np.where(df['HasClaim'] == 1, df['TotalClaims'], np.nan)

# Loss ratio and margin calculations
df['LossRatio'] = np.where(df['TotalPremium'] > 0, 
                            df['TotalClaims'] / df['TotalPremium'], 
                            np.nan)
df['Margin'] = df['TotalPremium'] - df['TotalClaims']

# Ensure ZipCode is string type for proper grouping
df['ZipCode'] = df['ZipCode'].astype(str)

Key Design Decision: We define `ClaimFrequency` as a binary indicator at the policy level (0 or 1), making it equivalent to the claim rate when aggregated across policies.

Test 1: Geographic Risk Assessment (Gauteng vs. Western Cape)

Hypothesis

  • Null Hypothesis (H₀): There is no difference in insurance risk metrics between Gauteng and Western Cape after controlling for vehicle and coverage characteristics.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): Significant differences exist in claim patterns between these provinces.

Control Variables

To ensure fair comparison, we control for:

  • RegistrationYear: Vehicle age affects breakdown probability
  • Gender: Driver demographics
  • Make: Vehicle manufacturer (luxury vs. economy)
  • CoverCategory, CoverGroup, CoverType: Coverage level selections
  • TrackingDevice, AlarmImmobiliser: Security features
  • ExcessSelected: Deductible amount chosen
  • Section`, `Product`: Policy type

Propensity Score Model


from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.preprocessing import OneHotEncoder
from sklearn.compose import ColumnTransformer
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline

# Define control variables
controls = ['RegistrationYear', 'Gender', 'make', 'CoverCategory',
            'CoverGroup', 'CoverType', 'TrackingDevice', 'AlarmImmobiliser',
            'ExcessSelected', 'Section', 'Product']

# Separate categorical and numeric variables
categorical = ['Gender', 'make', 'CoverCategory', 'CoverGroup', 'CoverType',
               'TrackingDevice', 'AlarmImmobiliser', 'ExcessSelected', 
               'Section', 'Product']
numeric = ['RegistrationYear']

# Create preprocessing pipeline
preprocessor = ColumnTransformer([
    ('cat', OneHotEncoder(handle_unknown='ignore'), categorical),
    ('num', 'passthrough', numeric)
])

# Fit logistic regression: Gauteng = 1, Western Cape = 0
X = df[controls]
y = (df['Province'] == 'Gauteng').astype(int)

model = Pipeline([
    ('prep', preprocessor),
    ('logit', LogisticRegression(max_iter=5000, solver='saga'))
])
model.fit(X, y)

# Generate propensity scores and weights
df['pscore'] = model.predict_proba(X)[:, 1]
df['weight'] = np.where(df['Province'] == 'Gauteng', 
                         1 / df['pscore'], 
                         1 / (1 - df['pscore']))

Technical Note: We use the SAGA solver with 5000 iterations to ensure convergence for this high-dimensional problem (many categorical variables after one-hot encoding).

Weighted Mean Calculation


def weighted_mean(x, w):
    """Compute weighted average, ignoring NaNs."""
    mask = ~x.isna()
    return np.average(x[mask], weights=w[mask])

metrics = ['ClaimFrequency', 'ClaimSeverity', 'Margin']

weighted_results = (
    df.groupby('Province', group_keys=False)
      .apply(lambda g: pd.Series({
          m: weighted_mean(g[m], g['weight']) for m in metrics
      }))
      .reset_index()
)

print(weighted_results)

Statistical Significance Testing


from statsmodels.stats.weightstats import DescrStatsW, CompareMeans

results = {}
for metric in metrics:
    g1 = df[df['Province'] == 'Gauteng']
    g2 = df[df['Province'] == 'Western Cape']
    
    # Create weighted statistics objects
    d1 = DescrStatsW(g1[metric].dropna(), 
                     weights=g1['weight'].loc[g1[metric].notna()])
    d2 = DescrStatsW(g2[metric].dropna(), 
                     weights=g2['weight'].loc[g2[metric].notna()])
    
    # Perform weighted t-test (unequal variances)
    cm = CompareMeans(d1, d2)
    tstat, pvalue, _ = cm.ttest_ind(usevar='unequal')
    results[metric] = pvalue

print("\nWeighted T-test p-values:")
for m, p in results.items():
    print(f"{m}: p = {p:.4f}")

All three metrics show statistically significant differences (p < 0.05):

Hence we reject H₀ which states that there exists no diffrence in the risk metrics amomng provinces.

  1. Claim Frequency: Gauteng shows 49% higher claim frequency 3.29 per 1000 vs 2.21 per 1000 policies
    • Gauteng's urban density, traffic congestion, and larger population likely contribute
    • This translates to roughly 3-4 more claims per 1,000 policies annually
  2. Claim Severity: When claims occur, they're 16% less severe in Gauteng (R22,398 vs R26,819)
    • Possible explanations: more minor fender-benders in urban traffic vs. high-speed highway accidents in Western Cape
    • Lower average claim amounts partially offset the higher frequency
  3. Margin: Gauteng is significantly less profitable (-R13.41 vs -R1.53 per policy)
    • Both provinces show negative margins on average, but Gauteng is worse
    • The higher claim frequency dominates, making this a net unprofitable segment

Actionable Insight: Consider premium increases or stricter underwriting in Gauteng

Business Recommendation: Gauteng requires a differentiated pricing strategy. The combination of high frequency and low margin suggests current premiums don't adequately price for urban risk factors.

Enhanced Weighted Mean Function


def weighted_mean(x, w):
    """Compute weighted average safely, ignoring NaNs and zero weights."""
    mask = (~x.isna()) & (w > 0)
    if mask.sum() == 0:
        return np.nan  # no valid data
    return np.average(x[mask], weights=w[mask])

Policy and Ethical Considerations:

This analysis reveals a genuine, statistically robust difference in risk between genders. However, using gender for pricing raises important ethical and legal questions:

  • Fairness vs. Actuarial Accuracy: Is it fair to charge higher premiums based on a demographic characteristic individuals can't control?
  • Legal Constraints: Many jurisdictions prohibit gender-based pricing (e.g., EU since 2012)
  • Alternative Approaches:
    • Telematics and usage-based insurance can capture actual driving behavior
    • Vehicle choice and coverage selections already provide gender-correlated signals
    • Focus on controllable risk factors (security devices, driving courses)

Business Recommendation: Even where gender-based pricing is legal, consider the reputational and competitive implications. The industry trend is toward behavior-based pricing that achieves risk differentiation without demographic discrimination.

Technical Considerations and Limitations

Strengths of This Approach

  1. Causal Inference: Propensity score weighting controls for confounding, providing more reliable effect estimates than simple comparisons
  2. Policy-Level Analysis: Metrics calculated at the policy level avoid temporal aggregation issues
  3. Robust Testing: Weighted t-tests with unequal variance assumptions prevent false positives from heteroskedasticity

Limitations to Consider

  1. Unobserved Confounders: We can only control for variables in the dataset. Driving experience, commute distance, and annual mileage are not available but likely relevant
  2. Selection Bias: If certain customer types systematically choose or avoid our company, results may not generalize to the broader market
  3. Temporal Effects: This is a snapshot analysis. Seasonal patterns and trends over time aren't captured
  4. Model Convergence: Some propensity score models didn't fully converge, though results appear stable
  5. Sample Size Variation: Smaller segments (like "Not Specified" gender or rare zip codes) have wider confidence intervals

Recommendations for Further Analysis

  1. Longitudinal Study: Track cohorts over multiple years to assess claim pattern evolution
  2. Telematics Integration: Incorporate GPS and accelerometer data for behavior-based risk scoring
  3. Machine Learning: Gradient boosting or neural networks might capture non-linear interactions between risk factors
  4. External Data: Weather patterns, road quality indices, and local crime statistics could enhance geographic models
  5. Survival Analysis: Time-to-first-claim analysis could reveal differential early-policy risk
