Data Cleaning in Insurance Analytics

How I cleaned 376,719 zero-premium records and transformed messy operational data into a model-ready dataset.

Introduction

Let's talk about the part of data science that nobody puts on their resume: data cleaning.

You've probably heard the stat: data scientists spend 80% of their time cleaning data and only 20% on analysis and modeling. When I started my insurance analytics project, I thought that was an exaggeration. I was wrong.

The first time I loaded the raw dataset, I discovered 376,719 policy records with zero premium values. Not missing values but actual zeros. My initial reaction was panic: "Is this entire dataset corrupted?" The investigation that followed taught me more about data quality than any textbook ever could.

In this post, I'll walk you through the real challenges of cleaning operational insurance data the kind of messy, inconsistent, real-world data that most tutorials conveniently skip. If you're working with data from production systems and not sanitized Kaggle datasets , this is for you.

The 80/20 Rule of Data Science

Before diving into the technical details, let's acknowledge an uncomfortable truth: the greatest machine learning algorithm is useless if your data is garbage.

I've seen countless projects where data scientists rush to model building, only to discover later that their impressive accuracy scores were built on faulty foundations. Missing values treated as zeros. Duplicates inflating sample sizes. Categorical variables with a dozen variations of the same category.

This project forced me to slow down and get the fundamentals right. And honestly? The data cleaning phase taught me more about insurance business logic than any analysis could have.

Challenge 3: Standardizing Categoricla Variables

The Nightmare Scenario

Categorical variables are the backbone of insurance analytics, but they were an absolute mess.

The Problem:

The same bank appeared as 12 different values due to formatting inconsistencies. Multiply this across all categorical columns, and you have a cardinality explosion that would destroy any model.

Why This Happens:

Operational databases accumulate formatting inconsistencies over years:

  • Manual data entry with varying capitalization
  • System migrations that didn't clean legacy data
  • Different validation rules across different periods
  • Copy-paste errors from external sources

Challenge 4: The Zero Permium Issue

The Discovery

  • One of the most alarming discoveries came when validating numerical fields:
  • 376,719 policies with zero premium. That's not a typo. Over one-third of the dataset had no premium amount.

The Investigation

Initial panic subsided as I investigated systematically:

Question 1: Are these data entry errors?

  • The absence of negative values suggested data entry controls were working. But zero premiums?

Question 2: Could these be legitimate?

Possible legitimate scenarios:

  • Promotional policies: Free insurance for a limited time
  • Complementary coverage: Free add-ons to existing policies
  • Waived premiums: Customer loyalty programs
  • Pending policies: Not yet priced by underwriters
  • Cancelled policies: Reversed before charging

Question 3: What about policies with claims but no premium?

  • This is where it gets problematic:
  • 45,632 policies paid out claims without collecting premiums. This violates basic insurance logic you can't claim on a policy you didn't pay for.

The Solution: Data Quality Flags

Rather than deleting these records immediately, I created a flag for filtering:

This approach:

  • Preserves original data: Nothing is deleted, just flagged
  • Documents the decision: Clear logic for what's considered invalid
  • Enables investigation: Business analysts can review flagged records
  • Maintains flexibility: Easy to adjust the filter criteria later

Business Consultation

I documented this finding and recommended:

  1. Review with actuarial team: Understand legitimate zero-premium scenarios
  2. System investigation: Check if these are export artifacts
  3. Policy review: Manually inspect sample of flagged records
  4. Process improvement: Add validation at point of data entry

Lesson: When you find anomalies, don't just delete them. Investigate, document, and involve domain experts. The anomaly might reveal a business process issue, not just a data issue.

Final Thoughts

  • Data cleaning isn't glamorous. It doesn't make for impressive conference presentations. But it's absolutely critical.
  • The 376,719 zero-premium records could have silently destroyed every model I built. The inconsistent categorical formatting would have created artificial features that confused algorithms. The missing gender values would have forced me to discard valuable data.
  • Time spent cleaning: ~3 weeks
  • Time saved in debugging bad models: Immeasurable
  • If you take one thing from this post, let it be this: respect the cleaning phase. Rush through it, and you'll pay for it later. Do it right, and everything that follows becomes easier.
  • Your models are only as good as your data. Clean data isn't everything, but everything is nothing without clean data.
