The Business Challenge

Insurance pricing is one of the oldest risk assessment problems in business, yet it remains incredibly complex. Actuaries have traditionally relied on statistical models and domain expertise to set premiums, but modern datasets offer opportunities that classical methods often miss.

Core Challenges

  • Risk Assessment Complexity: Insurance pricing requires balancing accurate risk assessment with competitive market positioning. Price too high, and you lose customers to competitors. Price too low, and you expose the company to losses.
  • Data Quality Issues: Operational insurance databases are messy. Years of system migrations, manual data entry, and evolving business rules create inconsistencies that must be resolved before any meaningful analysis can begin.
  • Customer Expectations: In today's digital economy, customers expect instant quotes. The days of waiting for an underwriter to manually review your application are over.
  • Hidden Patterns: Traditional actuarial models excel at capturing known relationships but may miss nuanced patterns buried in millions of policy records patterns that machine learning can uncover.

I designed and implemented an end-to-end analytics system that addresses these challenges through a systematic, data-driven approach. The project follows the CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining) framework, adapted specifically for the insurance domain.

The Dataset: Insurance policy records containing claims information, premium amounts, customer demographics, vehicle characteristics, and coverage details from thousands of policies.

Tech Stack

  • Python: for all data processing and modeling
  • pandas & numpy: for data manipulation
  • matplotlib & seaborn: for visualization
  • scikit-learn: for machine learning pipelines
  • XGBoost: for gradient boosting models
  • scipy: for statistical testing
  • joblib: for model persistence

The Deliverable: A production-ready premium prediction system that processes new customer data and returns accurate, instant quotes.

Data Cleaning and Preprocessing

Data cleaning is where data science gets real.

The raw dataset arrived as `MachineLearningRating_v3.txt`—an export from an operational insurance database. The first challenge? Even loading the file required special handling due to non-standard delimiters.

Immediately, I encountered several categories of data quality issues that needed systematic remediation.

This raised critical questions: Were these data entry errors? Promotional policies? Incomplete records? The investigation revealed that while no negative values existed, the zero premiums required deeper business logic analysis.

"From 500+ features to 50 while retaining 95% of the information."

After validating provincial differences, I turned to feature engineering for machine learning. But there was a problem: "the curse of dimensionality".

The high-cardinality challenge

After one-hot encoding categorical variables:

  • ZipCode: 200+ unique values → 200+ binary features
  • Make: 50+ unique values → 50+ binary features
  • Province, CoverCategory, etc.: Combined, over 500 features

Consequences:

  • Massive overfitting risk
  • Training time measured in hours
  • Memory constraints
  • Model interpretability nightmare

The PCA Solution

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) transforms correlated features into uncorrelated components that capture maximum variance. I applied PCA column-by-column to maintain semantic meaning:

Pipeline Architecture: OneHotEncoder → RobustScaler → PCA

Dimensionality Reduction

  • Province: 9 categories → 4 principal components
  • Make: 50+ categories → 12 principal components
  • ZipCode: 200+ categories → 20 principal components
  • CoverCategory: 15 categories → 6 principal components
  • Total: From 500+ features to approximately 55 features while retaining 95% of variance.

Impact on model preformance

Training Efficiency:

  • Before PCA: 45 minutes training time
  • After PCA: 8 minutes ~ 82% reduction

Model Performance:

  • Reduced overfitting
  • Eliminated multicollinearity
  • Slightly improved cross-validation scores (noise reduction)

Production Benefits:

  • Model file size: 250 MB → 45 MB
  • Faster inference for real-time predictions
  • Easier deployment and versioning

Predictive Modeling

"Two models are better than one: Separating probability from severity."

With optimized features, I was ready to build the predictive models. But rather than a single model predicting premiums directly, I implemented a dual-model architecture that mirrors actuarial frequency-severity modeling.

The Dual-Model Philosophy

Why two models?

Insurance premiums fundamentally depend on two factors:

  1. How likely is a claim? (Frequency/Probability)
  2. How expensive will it be? (Severity/Amount)

By modeling these separately:

  • Each model can optimize for its specific task
  • Better handling of zero-inflated claim data
  • Clearer business interpretation
  • Separate tuning strategies

Clam Probablity

  • Objective: Predict whether a policy will result in a claim.
  • Target Engineering:
    • The Class Imbalance Problem: With 85% negative cases, models would simply predict "no claim" for everyone and achieve 85% accuracy while being completely useless.
  • Solution: SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique)

Leason Learned

After months of work on this comprehensive project, several key lessons emerged:

1. Data Quality is Paramont

The time invested in thorough data cleaning paid massive dividends in model performance. Garbage in, garbage out isn't just a cliché it's reality. The 376,719 zero-premium records could have silently corrupted every downstream analysis if not properly investigated and handled.

2. Domain Knowledge Matters

Understanding insurance business logic was crucial for:

  • Interpreting zero premiums correctly
  • Designing the frequency-severity dual model
  • Setting appropriate business loadings
  • Identifying invalid data patterns
  • Communicating results to stakeholders

3. Class Imbalance is Real

SMOTE wasn't optional it was critical. Without addressing the 85/15 class split in claim occurrence, the models would have been practically useless despite appearing to achieve high accuracy.

4. Separation of Concerns

The dual modeling approach (classification + regression) consistently outperformed single unified models. Each model could optimize for its specific objective without compromise.

5. Statistical Rigor Builds confidence

The A/B testing framework wasn't just academically interesting it provided the statistical evidence needed to justify real business decisions. Correlation is interesting; proven causation (after controlling for confounders) is actionable.

6. Production is Different from Prototyping

Building models in Jupyter is one thing. Creating a system that handles edge cases, validates inputs, manages model versions, and provides clear outputs requires different thinking. The production gap is real.

7. Feature Engineering > Algorithm selection

PCA and proper feature transformation had more impact on final performance than choosing between Random Forest and XGBoost. Good features make every algorithm perform better.

8. Documentaion is an Investment

Every data cleaning decision, every modeling choice, every hyperparameter setting was documented. Six months later, this documentation is invaluable when questions arise or updates are needed.
